\doxysubsection{Detailed Description}
NOTE:\+ 3/\+30/\+17. 

NOTE:\+ 3/\+30/\+17. This is tailored for constructions associated with ORNL and 3Dprinting and should be easy to convert to C++ or matlab. The original code started in \textquotesingle{}Hex\+Platen\textquotesingle{}, but has been improved and streamlined.


\begin{DoxyItemize}
\item Intensities will be interpolated from data provided in a file:\+ the intensity "{}field"{} has been a rectangular grid, 0 intensity outside; TOTO:\+ we need more general methods of getting intensities.
\item The intensity file or the user should also specify:\+ \textquotesingle{}center\textquotesingle{} and \textquotesingle{}angle\textquotesingle{} for positioning the microgrid.
\item Shape will be cut out after construction instead of before.
\item Shape/\+intensity data are left in their real-\/world locations; our microgrid is scaled, translated, rotated to match.
\end{DoxyItemize}

Motivation:\+ creating circle packings whose duals can be used as 2D grids for 3D printer head planning. Started in response to inquiries by Greg Dreifus while he was at ORNL\textquotesingle{}s, carried on with John Bowers (JMU), Pum Kim, and others at ORNL MDF.

Potential advantages:\+ $\ast$ can use triangulation or dual; $\ast$ can manipulate (within limits) to change the shape; $\ast$ connection to analytic/\+harmonic functions -- e.\+g., heat dissipation $\ast$ dual graph generically has trivalent vertices, good for rigidity.

Aim Here:\+ Use interconnected hex lattices so we can change circle sizes to accommodate different strength/\+density requirements. However, we will have to use overlaps and inversive distances mixed in with the usual tangencies. The idea is to allow selective refinement but still maintain ability to manipulate with conformal-\/like behavior.

At the base is a "{}microgrid"{} --- several hex generations around the shape center forming a global hex pattern. Within that we define "{}supergrids"{}, uniform hex lattices chosen from the microgrid (always including the center as a supergrid vertex). The numerical "{}diameter"{} of the microgrid is 1, while supergrids have integral diameters.

The underlying packing always represents the full microgrid, but it is scaled by \textquotesingle{}micro\+Scaling\textquotesingle{} (so its radii are \textquotesingle{}micro\+Scaling\textquotesingle{}/\+2.0). Only selected centers will end up being used; the data is kept in arrays of \textquotesingle{}Node\textquotesingle{}s, which know \textquotesingle{}my\+Vert\textquotesingle{} (their index in the microgrid), their integral diameters, and other pertinent data.

{\footnotesize\ttfamily \label{classmicro_lattice_1_1_micro_grid_acc944c83e25b51095da7a30145b1f5e0} 
int micro\+Lattice.\+Micro\+Grid.\+process\+Level (\begin{DoxyParamCaption}\item[{int}]{level}{, }\item[{int}]{step}{}\end{DoxyParamCaption})}



This is the heart of the current algorithm, although it is still under development. 

Currently each level is a 5-\/stage process, with circles possibly chosen at stages 1, 3, and 4; other steps help keep track of chosen and exclusions in \textquotesingle{}processed\textquotesingle{}.

(1) We choose vertices at this level, based on \textquotesingle{}micro\+Intensity\textquotesingle{} and \textquotesingle{}processed\textquotesingle{} exclusion info. Thus, if qualified by intensity and not chosen or excluded at this or lesser level, then choose and record nix\+Large[level] stencil exclusions as -\/(level+1) in \textquotesingle{}processed\textquotesingle{}.

(2) We want a barrier around the components of \textquotesingle{}level\textquotesingle{} verts identified in Step 1. The idea? To prevent \textquotesingle{}level\textquotesingle{} verts from getting nghbs at any level higher than \textquotesingle{}level+1\textquotesingle{}; to allow addition of \textquotesingle{}level\textquotesingle{} verts filling some of the gap formed by the new \textquotesingle{}level+1\textquotesingle{}s; and yet to prevent \textquotesingle{}level\textquotesingle{} vert choices from spilling across the barrier into regions that should have larger circles. In this step we compile a vector of coords (U,V) for the \textquotesingle{}level+1\textquotesingle{} superlattice to surround components of \textquotesingle{}level\textquotesingle{}. Not easy:\+ we create a new packing (put in p2 for debug purposes) representing the \textquotesingle{}level+1\textquotesingle{} superlattice and with vertex\+Map back to the micro\+Grid. We mark faces having a micro\+Grid vert which is chosen or excluded at -\/(level+1), then create the \textquotesingle{}barrier\textquotesingle{} vector of \textquotesingle{}level+1\textquotesingle{} verts having at least one but not all its faces marked.

(3) Now we construct the barrier itself, which consists of chosen verts and verts excluded at \textquotesingle{}-\/level\textquotesingle{}. Passing through \textquotesingle{}barrier\textquotesingle{}, our (U,V) vector from Step 2, we set these as \textquotesingle{}chosen\textquotesingle{} at \textquotesingle{}level+1\textquotesingle{} and for them and any \textquotesingle{}ghost\textquotesingle{} nghbs we exclude \textquotesingle{}-\/level\textquotesingle{} neighborhood using their \textquotesingle{}nix\+Small[level+1]\textquotesingle{}. Next we use \textquotesingle{}nix\+Tang\+Pt[level]\textquotesingle{} to also exclude \textquotesingle{}-\/level\textquotesingle{} verts around tangency points of between chosen \textquotesingle{}level+1\textquotesingle{}s and/\+or ghosts. This is needed because otherwise, due to allowed overlaps, there can be leakage of \textquotesingle{}level\textquotesingle{} verts across near these tangency points.

(4) Look around boundary of current level to see if we should add more from the current level --- these didn\textquotesingle{}t qualify by intensity, but if they do not overlap those added in Step 3, choose them to help avoid big gaps.

(5) Finally, go back to all level+1 chosen in Step 2 and use \textquotesingle{}nix\+Small[level+1]\textquotesingle{} stencil to exclude at level+1.
